💧 Level 1 · Why Energy Now

Energy Transition for Beginners: what are the different types of clean energy available to SouthEast Asians?

If you're going to talk about the climate, you need to know your "products." You wouldn't sell skincare without knowing the ingredients, right?

The Energy Transition is basically a massive diet change for our countries. We're switching from "junk food" (Fossil Fuels: Coal, Oil, Gas) to "superfoods" (Renewables). Here's the cheat sheet for what's actually available in our backyard — Indonesia, Thailand, and the Philippines.

1. The "Superfoods" — Clean Energy Types

Know your menu before you start talking about it.

☀️
Solar — The People's Champ
What is it?

Energy from the sun. We have sun year-round — this is the most accessible option. You'll see Rooftop Solar (on houses & malls) and Floating Solar on reservoirs, like the Cirata plant in West Java or Sirindhorn Dam in Thailand.

💡 The cheapest form of new electricity in history.
🌋
Geothermal — Volcano Power
What is it?

Heat from under the earth. Indonesia and the Philippines are world leaders here because of the Ring of Fire. Reliable (works at night!), but expensive to drill — and can conflict with forests and indigenous lands if not handled carefully.

💨
Wind — The New Kid
What is it?

Giant fans that turn wind into power. We used to think our wind wasn't strong enough. Wrong. Vietnam is crushing it, and the Philippines and Indonesia (Sidrap & Jeneponto) are catching up fast.

💧
Hydro — The Old Guard
What is it?

Dams. Our oldest renewable source. Big dams are controversial — they can displace villages and hurt river ecosystems. Micro-hydro (tiny dams for one community) is the cooler, more community-friendly cousin.

🌾
Biomass — The Controversial One
What is it?

Burning organic waste (rice husks, palm oil shells) for power. Huge in Thailand. Some people call "burning wood pellets" (co-firing) renewable — but if it causes deforestation, it's definitely not cool.

⚠️ Keep a close eye on how biomass is sourced before you amplify it.

2. The Reality Check — Dreams vs. Data

Before you post "We're going 100% Green tomorrow!" — look at the numbers. Despite the hype, our countries are still heavily reliant on fossil fuels. Knowing the real picture makes your content credible.

The truth is more interesting than the hype. Share the real picture — it gives your audience something concrete to push for.

3. Credible Sources — Follow These

Don't just Google it. These organizations publish legit, citable data with charts you can screenshot and share with your audience.

Ember Climate ↗
Beautiful, easy-to-understand graphs about electricity systems globally and in SEA.
IESR — Institute for Essential Services Reform ↗
The go-to think tank for Indonesia's energy transition data.
ICSC — Institute for Climate and Sustainable Cities ↗
Great for Philippines context, focusing on fair and equitable energy policies.
Forum for the Future — Responsible Energy Initiative ↗
Multi-year programme ensuring renewable energy in Asia is ecologically safe, rights-respecting, and socially just. Philippines-focused.
Asia Research & Engagement ↗
Independent ESG-risk firm. Strong reports on financing the Asia energy transition.
ISEAS Yusof Ishak Institute ↗
Publishes studies on public opinion about climate change and accelerating the ASEAN energy transition.
IRID — Indonesia Research Institute for Decarbonisation ↗
Indonesian think tank focused on decarbonization and climate-resilient society.
World Resources Institute — Southeast Asia ↗
Global research org spanning 60+ countries, covering energy in Vietnam, Philippines, and Indonesia.
Stockholm Environment Institute — Asia ↗
Bangkok-based office bridging science, policy, and practice across Thailand and the region.
